Keyboard Sizes & Layouts

There are different Keyboard Sizes, this refers to the physical size of the keyboard and therefore the amount of keys it has. In general sizes are referred to in percentages: 100%, 75%, 40&, and so on, this usually uses a full-size layout as the reference point. The most common ones are full size / 100%, Tenkeyless / TKL, Compact / 60%, 75%, 65%, 40% , 1800 Layout. In smaller sizes there will be function layers that will allow for use of key that are not there.

Keycaps

There are different things that make keycaps what they are, including materials, profiles, and the way manufacturers make them, we will give you a brief explanation of what makes them different and we will also link external videos and pages that you can go to for more information regarding these things. Don't forget that when it comes to keyboards there's not such thing as "best"!, it's all personal taste, which is what makes this so unique.

Profiles

I. OEM profile: A profile with a cylindrical shape and a flat top surface, it is the most used profile and often considered the standard.
II. Cherry profile: A profile that is like OEM, but with a slightly lower height and a more aggressive slope.
III. SA profile: A tall profile with a spherical top surface, inspired by the classic Commodore 64 keyboard.
IV. DSA profile: A profile with a uniform height and a spherical top surface, it is popular for its minimalist design and ease of customization.
V. XDA profile: A profile with a uniform height and a flat top surface, it is like DSA but with a flatter profile.
VI. MT3 profile: A profile with a spherical top surface and a curved bottom, inspired by old terminal keyboards.

Switches

When it comes to switches there are some different things to keep in consideration, the "type" of switch, materials, lube, even films in some cases. But worry not! when you're first diving into the world of custom keyboards the main thing you will worry about is essentially the type of switch you use, and nowadays there are some switches that even come pre-lubed from the factory so you won't have to deal with the lubing part (in some cases at least).

Switch type

I. Linear switches: These switches have a smooth keystroke with no bump or tactile feedback. They are best for fast and precise typing.
II. Tactile switches: These switches have a small bump or tactile feedback when the actuation point is reached. They are best for a balance between typing speed and feedback.
III. Clicky switches: These switches have a tactile bump and an audible click sound when the actuation point is reached. They are best for a satisfying typing experience with audible feedback.

Parts of the switch

I. Top housing
II. Stem
III. Spring
IV. Bottom Housing
